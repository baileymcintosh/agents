## DETAILED ANALYSIS

### 1.1 Strategic Context & War Origins: From 12-Day War to Epic Fury

The conflict of February–March 2026 cannot be understood without its direct precursor: Israel's **Operation Rising Lion** of June 12–24, 2025, which this analysis will call the "12-Day War."

In June 2025, Israel launched an independent air campaign against Iran's nuclear infrastructure, achieving air superiority and destroying above-ground facilities at Natanz, Isfahan, and Fordow. IAEA verification reports from September 2025 confirmed that Fordow "suffered very significant damage" and that tunnel portal entrances at Natanz and Isfahan remained backfilled (ISIS-Online, November 2025 imagery analysis). US B-2 bombers, deploying GBU-57 Massive Ordnance Penetrators (MOPs), struck Fordow's ventilation shafts in what CSIS (2025) called the most significant kinetic attack on a hardened underground nuclear facility in history. However, CSIS analysis concluded: *"The strikes did not completely eliminate Iran's nuclear program."* Activity continued at Pickaxe Mountain (a lesser-known site), and an underground facility northeast of Isfahan — potentially Iran's fourth enrichment site — was not struck.

Critically, the 12-Day War left three strategic problems unresolved:

1. **The uranium stockpile was not located or destroyed.** Before June 2025, Iran held over 400kg of uranium enriched to 60% purity — a threshold that, with further enrichment to 90%+, could yield enough fissile material for multiple weapons. IAEA confirmed it lost visibility of this stockpile after the war; as of February 27, 2026, a confidential IAEA report stated it "does not know the location, size, or composition of Iran's enriched uranium stockpile" (Reuters, February 27, 2026).

2. **Iran's ballistic missile arsenal was untouched.** The 12-Day War deliberately excluded Iran's missile production and launch infrastructure, focusing exclusively on nuclear facilities. In the six months following, Iran reconstituted portions of its launch infrastructure and retained an estimated 1,000+ operational ballistic missiles of varying ranges.

3. **The regime survived and hardened.** The IRGC used the period after the 12-Day War to consolidate domestic control, suppress a wave of protests in December 2025–January 2026 (more than 7,000 killed in the crackdown, per PBS News/HRA Iran, February 2026), and implement decentralized command protocols in anticipation of a follow-on campaign.

By late January 2026, indirect US-Iran nuclear negotiations in Muscat, Oman (February 6, 2026) — mediated by Omani FM Badr bin Hamad Al Busaidi with US envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner — failed to produce agreement. Iran's delegation, led by FM Araghchi, offered "unprecedented concessions" on nuclear activities according to the Omani FM, but Trump demanded full dismantlement of enrichment capabilities, which Iran refused as non-negotiable. Trump signed executive orders imposing additional tariffs on Iranian trading partners. VP Vance expressed frustration that Supreme Leader Khamenei had not personally participated in negotiations.

The trigger for Operation Epic Fury was described by CENTCOM as a "trigger event conducted by the Israeli Defense Forces" occurring in the hours before the first US strikes at 1:15 AM ET on February 28. The precise trigger has not been publicly confirmed, but was likely an Israeli kinetic action in the preceding hours that set the pre-planned campaign into motion.

### 1.2 Operational Timeline: February 28 – March 16, 2026 (Days 1–17)

**DAY 1 — February 28, 2026 (The Opening Salvo)**

At 1:15 AM Eastern Time (9:45 AM Tehran), CENTCOM commenced Operation Epic Fury. More than 100 aircraft launched from land (B-52s, B-1Bs, B-2s from CONUS; F-15s, F-16s, F/A-18s from carrier groups) and sea (Tomahawk salvos from submarines and surface ships). Israel simultaneously launched **Operation Roaring Lion** — its parallel air campaign focused on Iran's air defense network and the remaining nuclear infrastructure.

In the first 24 hours, US-Israeli forces struck **over 1,000 targets** (CENTCOM/Army.mil, General Caine statement). Primary target categories: IRGC command and control facilities; Iranian air defense systems; ballistic missile launch sites; military airfields; naval assets; and — critically — the regime's senior leadership compound in Tehran, where a simultaneous gathering of senior political and military figures had been tracked by US-Israeli intelligence for months.

**Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was killed in the first hours of the operation.** Per reporting by the New York Times (cited by IranIntl), the NSA had tracked Khamenei for months and passed "high-fidelity" intelligence on his location to Israel ahead of the attack. US and Israeli officials adjusted the timing of their planned strike to exploit intelligence that senior political and military figures would gather at a leadership compound housing the offices of the supreme leader, the presidency, and the Supreme National Security Council.

Within hours of the opening strikes, **Iran launched its first retaliatory ballistic missile salvo**: IRGC forces fired missiles (including Emad, Ghadr, and the Fattah-1 hypersonic missile, Mach 15 capable, 1,400km range) against US bases in Qatar (Al Udeid), Bahrain (NSA Bahrain/Fifth Fleet HQ), UAE (Al Dhafra), and Kuwait (Al Salem). The IRGC claimed 200 casualties; the Pentagon confirmed **3 US service members killed and 5 seriously wounded** (Associated Press, March 1).

Also on Day 1: An accidental US or Israeli strike on a girls' school in Minab adjacent to a naval base killed approximately 170 civilians — a significant early PR crisis generating Congressional scrutiny and international condemnation.
