### Task 1: **The New Tariff Architecture — Mapping the August 2025 Regime and Its Second-Order Effects**

**Priority:** 1 (Highest)

**Rationale:**
The U.S. tariff regime has entered its most complex and consequential phase. Between August 1 and August 7, a cascade of new tariff actions takes effect simultaneously: country-specific reciprocal tariffs (with rates ranging from 10% to 50%), a 50% tariff on copper, a 35% tariff on Canadian goods under IEEPA/fentanyl authority, a 40% tariff on Brazil, and the first-ever "secondary tariffs" (targeting India for trade with Russia). This is no longer a bilateral trade dispute — it is a new global trade architecture being built in real time. Goldman Sachs estimates 37% of tariff costs are borne by U.S. consumers and 51% by U.S. businesses. The IMF projects global growth at 3.0% — "tenuous" — with tariffs as the primary downside risk. Morgan Stanley warns re-escalation to April peak rates "would likely spell a recession." Yet trade deals are also being struck (Japan at 15%, Philippines at 19%, Indonesia at 19%), creating winners and losers. This task requires a rigorous, sector-by-sector and country-by-country mapping of the new regime and its cascading economic effects.

**Expected Output:**
- A comprehensive tariff map (country × sector × rate × effective date) as of August 2025
- Analysis of supply chain disruption vectors (copper, steel/aluminum, autos, electronics, agriculture)
- Assessment of which economies face the most acute pressure (India, Brazil, EU, Canada, Mexico)
- Identification of arbitrage opportunities and transshipment risks (the new 40% anti-transshipment tariff)
- Scenario analysis: What happens if the July 9–August 7 deals hold vs. collapse
- Impact on U.S. inflation trajectory (CPI currently 2.4%, Fed projecting higher)

### Task 2: **Post-Ceasefire Middle East: Iran's Nuclear Calculus and the New Regional Security Architecture**

**Priority:** 2

**Rationale:**
The June 2025 "Twelve-Day War" between Israel, Iran, and the U.S. represents the most significant direct military confrontation between these powers in decades. The U.S. struck three Iranian nuclear facilities with bunker-buster bombs; Iran retaliated with missile attacks on Israeli cities (killing civilians in Beer Sheva); and a ceasefire was brokered by June 24 through Qatari mediation. However, the situation is deeply unstable. Iran's IRGC intelligence chief declared "the war has not ended" and Iran is in "a state of temporary pause." Iran demands guarantees against future attacks as a condition for a lasting agreement. Meanwhile, a parallel track to disarm Hezbollah (the Barrack proposal, with a December 31, 2025 deadline) is advancing but fragile. The Strait of Hormuz risk (20% of global oil transit) remains unresolved. This is the most consequential geopolitical flashpoint for energy markets, global growth, and defense posture.

**Expected Output:**
- Assessment of Iran's remaining nuclear capabilities post-strikes (what was destroyed, what remains)
- Analysis of Iran's strategic options: sprint to a weapon, negotiate, or proxy escalation
- Evaluation of the Hezbollah disarmament timeline and Israeli withdrawal conditions
- Oil market risk scenarios: probability-weighted Brent price trajectories under escalation/de-escalation
- Framework for understanding the new U.S.-Israel-Iran triangular deterrence structure
- Implications for Gulf state positioning (Saudi, UAE, Qatar) and global energy security

### Task 4: **The Fed's Impossible Position: Inflation, Fiscal Deficits, and the Tariff-Driven Stagflation Risk**

**Priority:** 4

**Rationale:**
The Federal Reserve faces a historically challenging policy environment. Rates are held at 4.25–4.50% with only two cuts expected by year-end (dot plot). But the cross-pressures are extraordinary: (a) Tariffs are pushing inflation higher — Morgan Stanley expects U.S. inflation to peak at 3.0–3.5% in Q3 2025 as companies pass through tariff costs; (b) Q1 GDP contracted at -0.5% annualized; (c) Treasury interest expense now exceeds defense spending, with massive refinancing needs at higher rates; (d) The "One Big Beautiful Bill" fiscal package will keep deficits wide, putting upward pressure on long-term yields; (e) Trump is intensifying political pressure on the Fed to cut rates, and reportedly fired the BLS Commissioner over unfavorable data. The 10-year yield has climbed 25–30 bps. J.P. Morgan maintains a 40% recession probability. This is a potential stagflation scenario that demands rigorous analysis of the Fed's decision tree and market implications.

**Expected Output:**
- Detailed analysis of the inflation pass-through from tariffs (by sector, by timeline)
- Assessment of the fiscal-monetary collision: deficit trajectory, refinancing schedule, yield curve dynamics
- Fed decision tree under scenarios: (a) inflation accelerates, (b) growth deteriorates, (c) both simultaneously
- Historical comparison to 1970s stagflation and 2022 tightening cycle
- Institutional risk analysis: Fed independence under political pressure, BLS credibility implications
- Market positioning implications: duration, credit, equities, dollar, gold
