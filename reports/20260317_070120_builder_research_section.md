## 1.1 How the War Began: Origins, Triggers & Strategic Objectives

### The Backstory: From Midnight Hammer to Epic Fury

To understand Operation Epic Fury, one must understand its predecessor. On June 22, 2025, the United States launched **Operation Midnight Hammer** — a B-2 strategic bomber and Tomahawk cruise missile campaign targeting Iran's three principal nuclear facilities at Fordow, Natanz, and Isfahan. This was the first direct US military attack on Iran's nuclear program and represented the Trump administration's decision that diplomacy had reached terminal failure.

The trigger was Iran's possession of approximately 460 kilograms of 60% enriched uranium — as confirmed by Special Envoy Steve Witkoff based on Iranian negotiators' own admission (Republican Policy Committee Background Memo, Feb. 28, 2026). At that enrichment level, Iran could achieve weapons-grade material in "seven to ten days," creating an effective breakout capability so compressed that deterrence-by-negotiation became untenable in US strategic calculus.

The Midnight Hammer strikes damaged above-ground infrastructure significantly but failed to penetrate deeply enough to destroy centrifuge halls and enriched uranium stocks. The IAEA confirmed in November 2025 assessments that Iran was using the eight-month interval to seal tunnel openings, accelerate work at the "Pickaxe Mountain" facility south of Natanz, and establish China-linked supply chains to rebuild. This reconstruction activity — proceeding in the shadow of a damaged but not-eliminated program — was, according to reporting, the proximate trigger for Epic Fury.

Before the February 28 strike, the US made a final pre-war offer: supply nuclear fuel free of charge on a long-term basis, removing Iran's stated need for domestic enrichment. Iran rejected this, insisting on retaining enrichment capabilities (Republican Policy Committee memo). The diplomatic off-ramp had closed.

### Operation Epic Fury: Stated and Actual Objectives

The White House's own statements reveal a shifting and partly contradictory set of objectives — a pattern that FlightGlobal's March 16 analysis sharply criticized as "the White House cycling through various rationales." A synthesis of stated objectives across multiple US government communications:

- **UN Ambassador Michael Waltz (JINSA update, March 1):** "Dismantling the regime's missile capabilities; interfering with its ability to arm its proxies; degrading its [nuclear program]"
- **Secretary Hegseth (Pentagon briefing, March 13):** "Defeat the missiles, missile launchers, and defense industrial base... defeat the navy, and deny Iran the ability to have a nuclear weapon"
- **White House videos (March 12-14):** Sequential stated objectives — destroy Iran's missiles, destroy Iran's navy, destroy terrorist proxies — suggesting an expanding or clarifying target set
- **Netanyahu (quoted in JINSA March 13 update):** "Setting the conditions that will allow the Iranian people to remove the cruel, tyrannical regime" — explicitly invoking regime change

The honest synthesis is that there are **three nested objectives** operating simultaneously, in increasing order of ambition and decreasing order of achievability:
1. **Nuclear disarmament** — destroying the enrichment program (achievable in theory, uncertain in practice)
2. **Military degradation** — destroying missile production, naval capacity, proxy supply chains (substantially achieved against fixed infrastructure, less against distributed capabilities)
3. **Regime change** — destabilizing the Islamic Republic sufficiently that the Iranian population overthrows it (historically the most ambitious and least reliable objective; not one that air campaigns have ever delivered)

The Trump administration has not formally declared regime change as an objective, but Netanyahu's explicit statements and the systematic targeting of IRGC Ground Forces units responsible for internal security suppression (ISW, March 12) — not militarily necessary to defeat the nuclear program — suggest regime change is the implicit third objective. This matters enormously for how Iran interprets the conflict and whether any negotiated endpoint is available.

**ANALYTICAL ASSESSMENT:** The war's original stated objectives (nuclear disarmament + missile program destruction) were achievable in a limited strike. The expansion to include proxy destruction, naval defeat, and de facto regime change signals have made a negotiated off-ramp structurally harder to construct. *The US has given Iran's new leadership every incentive to fight rather than negotiate, because any agreement short of regime survival would be seen as capitulation.* This is a classical strategic overreach pattern — analogous to the UN mandate expansion in Korea (1950) from defending South Korea to conquering North Korea, which produced Chinese intervention.

**Confidence: HIGH on the objective analysis; MEDIUM on whether this reflects deliberate policy or mission creep.**

## 1.2 The Military Campaign: What Has Been Struck and With What Effect

### 1.2a Nuclear & WMD Infrastructure

The nuclear program has been struck in two phases:

**Phase 1 — Operation Midnight Hammer (June 22, 2025):** US B-2 bombers dropped GBU-57 Massive Ordnance Penetrators (MOPs) on Fordow and Natanz; Tomahawk cruise missiles struck Isfahan. ISIS satellite imagery analysis (November 2025) showed "virtually zero activity" at all three sites. Transformers and power generators at Natanz were destroyed and had not been rebuilt. At Fordow, a penetration hole created by two GBU-57s above the buried enrichment hall was backfilled and capped — suggesting the underground portion was sealed, not destroyed. The DIA leaked assessment (CNN, June 2025) concluded the strikes were "largely restricted to aboveground structures" — critically, centrifuges and highly enriched uranium were not fully eliminated.

**Phase 2 — Operation Epic Fury (February 28, 2026 and ongoing):** IAEA confirmed "some recent damage" to Natanz buildings on March 3, with "no radiological consequences." Iran's own Foreign Ministry acknowledged nuclear facilities were "badly damaged, that's for sure." CENTCOM claims the overall campaign resulted in damage to Iran's nuclear capability that could "take years to rebuild." However, the February 2026 pre-war assessment (Alma Research Center, Israel) noted Iran had accelerated work at Pickaxe Mountain — a site south of Natanz that may contain centrifuges at great depth, potentially below the penetration threshold of even the GBU-57, and possibly unknown to IAEA inspectors.

**The Intelligence Gap Problem:** US officials themselves acknowledged to CNN that Iran "maintains secret nuclear facilities that were not targeted in the strike and remain operational." This is the unresolved strategic dilemma of both Midnight Hammer and Epic Fury: you can destroy known facilities thoroughly, but unknown facilities cannot be targeted. Iran's pre-war decision to pre-position highly enriched uranium stocks (IAEA confirmed material at Fordow, Natanz, and Isfahan, but the assessment was that Iran likely moved it before strikes) is the key variable.

**VERDICT:** The combined strikes have set back Iran's nuclear program by multiple years at declared facilities. They have not eliminated the program with certainty. The enriched uranium stockpile's location is the critical intelligence unknown. If Iran transferred ~460 kg of 60% HEU to Pickaxe Mountain or another undisclosed site, a theoretical weapons breakout capability survives.

**Confidence: MEDIUM — the uncertainty is genuine, not rhetorical hedging.**

### 1.2b Missile & Defense Industrial Base

Secretary Hegseth declared on March 12 that Iran's ballistic missile production is "functionally defeated." JINSA's March 13 update lists confirmed strikes on: Khojir Missile Complex (primary ballistic missile production), Parchin Military Complex (missile and possibly nuclear component testing), Shiraz Electronics Industries (guidance systems), and Hamadan (ongoing strikes as of March 16).

**Evidentiary assessment:** Iran has fired over 500 ballistic and naval missiles and nearly 2,000 drones since February 28 (Fars News, March 5 — notably from Iranian state media, which has incentive to report high numbers). The rate of ballistic missile launches declined measurably from the opening days — from 40 in the late evening of February 28 to a lower operational tempo afterward (JINSA, March 1 update). The ISW March 12 report noted Iranian missile units facing supply shortages.

The "functionally defeated" claim must be parsed carefully. Fixed production facilities, if destroyed, cannot be repaired quickly — Iranian solid-fuel missile production involves specialized equipment (autoclaves, casting facilities) that cannot be improvised. However:
- Iran entered the conflict with large pre-built missile stocks
- Iran's inventory in storage (not just what's been fired) is unknown
- "Functionally defeated" for production does not mean exhausted in existing inventory
- Iran has dispersed capabilities and could reconstitute elements in hardened underground facilities

**VERDICT:** Iran's missile production capacity has been severely degraded. Its existing inventory, while being depleted by the active campaign, remains substantial. Iran rationing missile use (as analysts have noted) suggests strategic conservation for a longer conflict, not imminent depletion.

**Confidence: HIGH on production degradation; LOW on inventory estimates.**
