## 3. Detailed Analysis

### 3A. The Twelve-Day War (June 13–24, 2025): What Happened

**Timeline of Events:**

- **June 12, 2025:** IAEA reports Iran in non-compliance with its Safeguards Agreement, citing undeclared material and activities suggesting weaponization pursuit.
- **June 13, 2025:** Israel launches a major preemptive strike against Iran, targeting nuclear facilities, ballistic missile sites, military infrastructure, and regime figures. Israel cited intelligence that Iran had "significantly advanced" toward a nuclear weapon.
- **June 13–21:** Israel conducts repeated strike waves. Targets include: Natanz pilot enrichment plant (above-ground components destroyed), Esfahan Nuclear Technology Center (uranium conversion complex hit three times, enriched uranium metal conversion plant destroyed), the IR-40 heavy water reactor at Arak (containment dome breached, reactor rendered inoperable), and numerous military/missile sites. Israel also conducted targeted assassinations of nuclear scientists and senior military commanders.
- **June 14–23:** Iran retaliates with ballistic missiles and drones against Israel. Most are intercepted. However, missiles hit residential buildings in Beersheba (4 killed) and other locations. 27 Israeli civilians killed in total (as of July 13, 2025).
- **June 22, 2025:** The United States joins the campaign with **Operation Midnight Hammer**. B-2 bombers and Tomahawk cruise missiles target Fordow (12 GBU-57 30,000-lb bunker busters dropped), Natanz, and Esfahan. Over 180 tons of bombs were dropped on Fordow alone, penetrating the mountain facility and destroying centrifuge cascade halls.
- **June 23, 2025:** Iran retaliates against the US by launching missiles at Al Udeid Air Base in Qatar. No American casualties reported. This was a critical signal — Iran targeted a US base but chose *not* to close the Strait of Hormuz or attack Gulf energy infrastructure.
- **June 24, 2025:** Ceasefire brokered by the US and Qatar. Structured as two 12-hour cessation periods. Despite violations in the opening hours (16 killed in Iran, 4 in Israel), the ceasefire held under US pressure.

**Casualties:** Iran reported approximately 1,062 killed (Iranian Health Ministry, as of July 22, 2025). Israel reported 29 killed (as of July 13, 2025). The asymmetry in casualties was roughly 36:1.

**Analytical Assessment:** The strikes destroyed Iran's operational enrichment infrastructure (Fordow centrifuges confirmed "no longer working" by Grossi; Natanz above-ground components destroyed; Esfahan conversion complex severely damaged). However, three critical elements survived: (1) Iran's stockpile of 972 lbs of 60%-enriched uranium — a "short, technical step" from weapons-grade; (2) potential undeclared centrifuge stocks and covert facilities; (3) institutional knowledge among surviving scientists. The strikes set back enrichment capacity by 1-2 years at a minimum but did not eliminate the nuclear threat. This distinction is crucial because it meant the underlying strategic problem was deferred, not resolved.

### 3B. The Interregnum (July 2025–February 2026): Diplomatic Failure

**Key Developments:**

- **July 2, 2025:** Iranian President Pezeshkian signs law suspending all cooperation with the IAEA. Inspectors are expelled.
- **July 4, 2025:** IAEA confirms inspectors have left Iran. The agency admits it "cannot verify whether Iran has suspended all enrichment-related activities" and cannot provide "any information on the current size, composition or whereabouts of the stockpile of enriched uranium."
- **September 2025:** IAEA and Iran reach a tentative agreement on inspections, but it provides no timeline for resumption. The E3 (UK, France, Germany) negotiate with Iran, offering to delay the "snapback" of UN sanctions in exchange for restored IAEA access.
- **September 28, 2025:** Snapback of UN Security Council resolutions from 2006-2010 takes effect after E3 triggers the mechanism. Iran threatens to further suspend cooperation.
- **October 2025:** Israel-Hamas ceasefire agreed (October 3). Houthis suspend Red Sea attacks. Signs emerge that Iran is rebuilding ballistic missile capabilities (European intelligence reports of chemical precursor shipments).
- **October-November 2025:** Satellite imagery shows repair activity at Natanz and Esfahan, though experts debate whether this represents reconstruction or damage assessment.
- **December 2025:** Nationwide anti-government protests erupt in Iran. Regime cracks down brutally. Trump deploys additional US forces and warns Khamenei to stop killing protesters.
- **December 24, 2025:** Iran formally rejects inspections of bombed nuclear sites until the IAEA defines "post-war conditions."
- **January-February 2026:** Five rounds of indirect US-Iran nuclear talks (mediated by Oman) produce no breakthrough. The fundamental gap: the US demands zero enrichment; Iran insists on its right to enrich. Iran offers to pause enrichment temporarily in exchange for sanctions relief; the US rejects this as insufficient.
- **February 24-26, 2026:** Senior US officials state they have "seen evidence" Iran is trying to rebuild its nuclear program. Trump warns of "bad things" if talks fail. Negotiations collapse on February 17 after Trump says Iran is "not willing to give us what we have to have."

**Analytical Assessment:** The diplomatic failure between June 2025 and February 2026 was structurally predictable. The June strikes degraded Iran's capabilities but hardened Iran's resolve. By expelling IAEA inspectors, Iran gained opacity — the ability to conduct any activity without verification. The snapback of UN sanctions further reduced Iran's incentive to cooperate, since it received the punishment of sanctions without any corresponding benefit. The US demand for "zero enrichment" — a maximalist position that no Iranian government could accept domestically — closed the diplomatic space. Each side's actions reinforced the other's worst-case interpretation, creating a classic security dilemma spiral.

- The Institute for Science and International Security assessed Iran retains knowledge remnants and could reconstitute capabilities "given enough time, investment, and dedication."
- Israeli intelligence assessed (per Le Monde) that Iran retains the *capability* to restart uranium enrichment.
- In November 2025, Iran's Deputy FM said Iran was "not enriching uranium."

**Assessment (Confidence: Moderate):** Iran's enrichment capacity was severely degraded twice (June 2025, February 2026) but not permanently eliminated. Iran retains: (a) stockpiles of enriched uranium of unknown current quantity/location, (b) institutional knowledge, (c) possible covert facilities. The expulsion of inspectors creates worst-case ambiguity. The most likely near-term trajectory is that Iran cannot produce a weapon in the next 6-12 months due to infrastructure destruction, but the long-term trajectory is **more dangerous** — Iran now has maximum motivation and minimal international oversight. Historical precedent (Iraq's Osirak strike, 1981) suggests military strikes on nuclear programs can delay but often *increase* a regime's determination to acquire weapons.
