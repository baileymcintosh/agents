## 3. Key Findings

**On the conflict itself:**
- **The US and Israel launched a surprise, large-scale air campaign on February 28, 2026**, striking over 5,000 targets in the first two weeks. The operation killed Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei on day one — the first time in the Islamic Republic's history that its top leader has been killed in a military strike.
- **Iran has retaliated broadly and persistently.** Approximately 2,000+ projectiles have been fired at Israel, Gulf states, and US military bases across at least nine countries. Iran has sustained ballistic missile launches against Israel throughout the conflict, including a strike on Jerusalem on March 16.
- **The Strait of Hormuz is effectively closed.** Over 150 tankers have diverted rather than risk transit. Insurance has been withdrawn. Qatar declared force majeure on all LNG shipments (approximately 20% of global LNG trade). The IEA authorized its largest-ever emergency reserve release — 400 million barrels — on March 11. Even with that release, oil is trading above $100 per barrel.
- **Israel opened a ground operation in Lebanon on March 16**, targeting Hezbollah positions south of the Litani River. Lebanon has sustained 850+ killed and nearly 1 million displaced — roughly 14% of its population.
- **The conflict has simultaneously closed two global shipping chokepoints** — the Strait of Hormuz and the Red Sea (where Houthi attacks resumed February 28). This is without modern precedent.

**On Iran's leadership:**
- Mojtaba Khamenei, 56, was named the new Supreme Leader on March 8–9. He is a hardline IRGC-connected figure with deep institutional ties but **no public profile** and no record of holding government office. He has not appeared in person or on video since his appointment. Early reports indicate he was injured in the February 28 strike that killed his father, mother, wife, and son.
- His first public statement, delivered in writing on March 12, was entirely defiant: he vowed continued military resistance, called for the Strait of Hormuz to remain closed as a "tool of pressure," and demanded the closure of all US bases in the region.
- Real operational command appears to rest with the IRGC, which is acting on pre-delegated authorities established before the war began.

**On the nuclear question — the most consequential unresolved issue:**
- Iran's nuclear facilities at Natanz and Isfahan have been struck, but the 400+ kilograms of 60%-enriched uranium (enough material for approximately 10 nuclear weapons if further enriched) **has not been destroyed and its exact location is unknown.** The IAEA confirmed it no longer has access to Iran's enrichment facilities.
- The US is reportedly exploring ground force options — potentially JSOC special operations in coordination with Israeli commandos — to physically secure or destroy the underground Isfahan tunnels where much of the uranium is believed to be stored. No ground operation has been announced.

**On diplomacy:**
- **There are no ceasefire talks.** The Trump administration formally rejected mediation efforts by Oman and Egypt (Reuters, March 14). Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi stated publicly on March 15: *"We have never asked even for negotiation."* Trump said Iran "appears ready to make a deal" but that terms are not good enough — a claim Iran immediately denied.
- The International Crisis Group concluded on March 16 that "neither side is positioned for a decisive win," making the escalation risk increasingly serious.

The Builder identified five scenarios; the three most probable are summarized here. Full probability modeling is planned for Cycle 4.

US and Israel achieve their stated military objectives within 4–6 total weeks; a face-saving framework is brokered allowing both sides to claim success; the Strait of Hormuz reopens. This requires a political shift that is not currently visible. Oil retreats toward $75–80/bbl.

**Scenario B — Extended Air Campaign, Partial Reopening (Estimated probability: 35–40%)**
The bombing continues for 2–4 more months with no ground invasion. The US organizes a naval escort coalition that partially reopens Hormuz. Iran sustains damage but the regime does not collapse. Oil stabilizes in the $85–100 range. This is the market's current base case, reflected in the modest equity pullback seen so far.

**Scenario C — Broader Regional War (Estimated probability: 15–20%)**
The Lebanon ground operation escalates; Gulf states are drawn into active combat; Iranian strikes cause major infrastructure damage in Saudi Arabia or the UAE; the conflict becomes a multi-actor regional war. Oil spikes toward $130–150/bbl. Global growth materially impairs.

**Critical wild card:** Iran's enriched uranium stockpile. If Iran moves toward nuclear assembly or announces a weapons test, every scenario collapses into a new and more dangerous framework that this cycle's research did not model.
